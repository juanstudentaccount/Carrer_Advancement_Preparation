Career Advancement Preparation Charter

A semester-long plan to move from CIS student to entry-level IT help desk professional by combining project management principles with real-world deliverables, networking, and continuous learning.

Primary Outcome

Build a repeatable system for career growth—resume, skills, certifications, and interview stories—so that after this course I am fully prepared to secure an entry-level IT help desk role.

01 · Project Overview

What this project is about

The Career Advancement Preparation project is a structured, semester-long initiative that treats my career development as a project. Instead of building a single technical system, the project focuses on creating a set of professional deliverables—including a refined resume, completed certification, interview stories, and networking conversations—that all support my goal of entering the IT help desk field.

Throughout the semester I will apply project management concepts such as scope, schedule, risk, and stakeholder management to my own career. Weekly status reports, a hybrid methodology, and a Trello board will help keep work organized, visible, and aligned with my goal.

02 · Mission & Vision

Purpose and future state

Mission: Build a strong foundation of technical, professional, and interpersonal skills that prepares me for an entry-level IT help desk role while practicing real project management techniques.

Vision (3–5 years): Grow from an entry-level IT help desk technician into a cybersecurity professional who supports secure, resilient systems and mentors other students beginning their own IT careers.

Growth Mindset Long-Term Career Roadmap Project-Based Learning
03 · Goals & Deliverables

What success looks like

  • Submit at least ten targeted applications for entry-level IT help desk roles.
  • Complete the Google IT Support Professional Certificate to strengthen my technical foundation.
  • Conduct informational interviews with IT and cybersecurity professionals to build my network.
  • Create a polished project hub website showcasing my charter, status reports, interview, and STAR stories.
  • Develop at least six STAR stories demonstrating teamwork, problem-solving, quality, resilience, and stewardship.
04 · Stakeholders

Who is involved

  • Primary stakeholder: Myself, as the project owner and beneficiary.
  • Instructor & classmates: Provide feedback, accountability, and peer learning.
  • Industry professionals: Interview partners and networking contacts who share real-world expectations.
  • Future employers: Indirect stakeholders that will later evaluate the skills and artifacts produced.

05 · Hybrid Iterative Methodology

The project uses a hybrid approach: a semester-long plan with clear milestones (similar to waterfall), combined with weekly check-ins and adjustments (similar to agile). Each “release” focuses on a specific set of outcomes such as updating the resume, completing certification modules, preparing interview stories, or finalizing the closure document and STAR stories.

06 · Tools & Resources

Systems supporting the project

  • Trello board: Tracks tasks, milestones, and weekly status using columns such as Backlog, In Progress, and Done.
  • Google IT Support Professional Certificate: Provides structured technical learning and hands-on labs.
  • GitHub Pages: Hosts the project hub, charter, status reports, interview summary, and STAR stories in one place.
  • Career resources: LinkedIn, job boards, and interview preparation materials to align work with real opportunities.
Trello Google IT Support GitHub Pages

07 · Visual Task Management

Trello keeps the project visible and manageable. Each card represents a concrete task— such as “apply to a specific job posting” or “complete a module of the Google IT Support Certificate”—with due dates, labels, and notes so nothing falls through the cracks.

08 · Risks & Constraints

What could get in the way

  • Time and workload: Balancing multiple CIS courses with labs, group projects, and this career-focused work.
  • Delayed responses from professionals: Reaching out for interviews and networking can take longer than expected.
  • Motivation & burnout: Keeping energy and focus through a demanding semester with several deadlines.

To reduce these risks, I use weekly planning, smaller milestones, and open communication with classmates and my instructor. If something slips, the hybrid plan allows me to re-balance priorities and move less critical tasks into later weeks.

09 · Quality & Success Criteria

How quality is built in

  • Each deliverable (charter, STAR stories, interview summary, status reports) is reviewed against the rubric and revised at least once.
  • Links, formatting, and content on the project hub are tested for clarity, consistency, and usability.
  • STAR stories follow a clear Situation–Task–Action–Result structure and connect to PMI principles.
  • Reflection after major milestones captures lessons learned and improvement opportunities for future projects.
10 · Reflection

What I learned from this project

This project showed that project management is more than tools—it is a mindset. Treating my own career as a project helped me understand how scope, schedule, risk, and stakeholders connect to real life. Weekly status reports pushed me to be honest about progress, and they made it easier to adapt when things did not go exactly as planned.

I also learned how valuable it is to keep everything visible. The hub, Trello board, status reports, and STAR stories all give me evidence of my effort, which I can re-use in interviews and future classes.

11 · Key Takeaways

Insights for future projects

  • Start with a realistic scope and adjust early when the original plan is too ambitious.
  • Schedule regular check-ins (like weekly status reports) to maintain momentum and accountability.
  • Build buffer time for learning new tools, certifications, and technologies instead of assuming everything will go perfectly.
  • Use visual tools like Trello to keep work organized and reduce stress when juggling many tasks.
  • Treat networking and interviews as project tasks—not afterthoughts—so they receive time and attention.

These lessons will carry over into future technical, academic, and career projects—especially as I move toward cybersecurity and more advanced IT roles.
